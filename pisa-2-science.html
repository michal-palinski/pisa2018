Relative performance of low- and high-achieving students
Figure compares scores at the 10th percentile of science performance distribution across the countries with the largest education systems. One can note that even low-achieving students in the four Chinese provinces outperform most students in other countries scoring around 480 points on the PISA scale. In other top-achieving countries, the weakest students also perform quite high, e.g., above 390 points in Japan, Canada and Poland, or above 380 points in Korea, or Chinese Taipei. In Saudi Arabia, but also in Morocco, Brazil, and Argentina, low-achieving students perform at very low levels around 290 score points, meaning that they lack basic science skills and are not able to answer most of the PISA test science items. These students demonstrate science skills level that is highly unsatisfactory, considering that they spent nearly 10 years in formal education. Similarly low are scores of the weakest students in the United Arab Emirates (around 300 points at the 10th percentile) and slightly better in Jordan (315 points).

Figure shows similar comparisons for the highest-achieving students, i.e., those with science scores at the 90th percentile for a country distribution. A list of countries at the top is slightly different now. Again, students from China, Japan, Korea, Taipei, and Canada are in the top ranking positions among the best students from the larger education systems. However, the performance of the best-achieving students in Germany, Australia, Poland, or the United States is similarly high. Interestingly, the gap between the best students in China and other countries is smaller than for the weakest students, but still they outperform the best students in all these high-performing countries by a margin of 50 score points or larger, which is half of the standard deviation or more. The best students from China and from the United States, for example, perform at 695 and 630 score points, which is a substantial gap that shows how excellent is knowledge and science skills of the best Chinese students. Thus, at least in the four Chinese provinces, education system can assure that almost none of the students lacks basic science skills at the age of 15, and their best students outperform peers in other large education systems by a substantial margin.
