Gender gap in reading achievement
Girls outperform boys in reading in all international studies and across time and cultures. In the very first edition of PISA in 2000, it was already obvious that on average across the OECD countries the advantage of girls in reading is substantial. This result was later confirmed in each PISA study, but also by studies in primary school, like PIRLS. The gap disappears, however, among adults, but the mechanisms behind this change are not well understood (see:" %} link). In any case, limited reading skills pose a barrier for learning not only in national language classes. Students who are not fluent readers have more limited access to knowledge and have difficulties progressing through textbooks in other subjects. Thus, lack of reading skills which is more prevalent among boys can create a {% trans "barrier for their education and increase gaps in other subjects.

As shown on Figure below, in PISA 2018 the gender gaps in reading performance are the largest in United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia and Jordan. Girls in Saudi Arabia and Jordan outperform boys by more than 50 score points, which is an equivalent to around two years of formal education. This gap is twice as large as on the average across the OECD countries. These results confirm earlier findings from studies like PIRLS, which demonstrated that already in primary school the gap in reading is much larger in Saudi Arabia and neighboring countries when compared to, for example, European countries or the United States.

The gender reading gap is also relatively large in Poland and Canada - two large education systems that perform at the top of the PISA reading rankings. In most countries, however, the gap is around 20-25 score points, which is equivalent to around one year of education. Among top performing countries the smallest gap is measured across the four Chinese provinces where girls outperform boys by only 13 score points and both genders demonstrate very high level of reading skills. The smallest gender gaps are found in South America, e.g. in Colombia, Peru, Mexico, Argentina, and Chile.
